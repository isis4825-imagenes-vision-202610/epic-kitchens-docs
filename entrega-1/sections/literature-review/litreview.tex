\section{State of the Art}

Multimodal video-text retrieval has seen accelerated advances in recent years, driven by the convergence of three research lines: contrastive learning, vision-language foundation models, and first-person video understanding. This section reviews the most relevant works organized around three thematic axes: (i)~adaptation of vision-language models for video retrieval, (ii)~multimodal pretraining in the egocentric domain, and (iii)~multi-instance retrieval with graded relevance.

\subsection*{Adaptation of vision-language models for video retrieval}

Contrastive image-text pretraining models learn shared representation spaces from large collections of image-text pairs and laid the groundwork for multimodal retrieval with video. Luo et al.~proposed CLIP4Clip, an empirical study that adapts this class of models to video clip retrieval through three temporal aggregation strategies: frame representation averaging, sequential transformers, and tight coupling between encoders~\cite{luo2022clip4clip}. Their experiments on MSR-VTT, MSVD, and ActivityNet Captions showed that the quality of pretrained representations outweighs the choice of temporal aggregation strategy, reinforcing the central role of the shared representation space. Ma et al.~extended this paradigm with X-CLIP, which introduces multi-granularity contrastive learning capable of simultaneously aligning video-sentence pairs at the global level and frame-word pairs at the local level, via a cross-frame communication transformer~\cite{ma2022xclip}. This fine-grained correspondence capture represents a notable improvement over single-level alignment methods. In a complementary direction, Rasheed et al.~demonstrated with ViFi-CLIP that end-to-end fine-tuning of vision-language models for video, with minimal architectural modifications, achieves competitive results in zero-shot generalization~\cite{rasheed2023vificlip}, showing that shared embedding spaces have a high transfer capacity to new domains.

\subsection*{Multimodal pretraining in the egocentric domain}

First-person video presents particular challenges: dynamic camera motion, high intra-class variability in everyday actions, and a data distribution very different from third-person video. The corpus that enabled large-scale pretraining in this domain is Ego4D, collected by Grauman et al.~in a collaboration of thirteen universities and Meta AI~\cite{grauman2022ego4d}. With over 3,600 hours of first-person video recorded by 931 participants in nine countries, Ego4D established the data infrastructure on which the most recent egocentric models are built. Damen et al.~introduced EPIC-KITCHENS-100, the reference dataset for egocentric cooking activities, which also establishes the multi-instance retrieval (MIR) task based on relevance matrices with soft labels~\cite{damen2022rescaling}. This formulation recognizes that multiple video segments may be relevant to the same textual query at different degrees of semantic similarity, requiring order-sensitive evaluation metrics (such as nDCG and mAP) rather than the traditional R@K.

To capitalize on the availability of the Ego4D corpus, Lin et al.~presented EgoVLP, the first video-language pretraining model designed specifically for the egocentric domain~\cite{lin2022egovlp}. EgoVLP introduces EgoNCE, a variant of InfoNCE that weights additional positive pairs through verb and noun matching, and achieves substantial improvements over general-domain methods on EK-100. However, the handling of multiple instances in EgoNCE relies on lexical overlap rather than directly leveraging the official graded relevance matrices of the challenge. Zhao et al.~advanced in this direction with LAVILA, which employs a language model fine-tuned on Ego4D to generate diverse narrations of training clips, enriching the available semantic supervision~\cite{zhao2023lavila}. LAVILA achieved state-of-the-art results on EK-100 MIR at the time of publication and became the reference video encoder for subsequent works. Pramanick et al.~proposed EgoVLPv2, which integrates cross-modal fusion directly into the network backbone via cross-attention layers interleaved with self-attention layers~\cite{pramanick2023egovlpv2}, obtaining richer joint representations by capturing temporal and semantic dependencies in an integrated manner.

\subsection*{Multi-instance retrieval, graded relevance, and foundation models}

The official EK-100 MIR challenge baseline combines two models provided by the organizers. The first is JPoSE, proposed by Wray et al., which learns separate embedding spaces for verbs, nouns, and full actions, combining them for fine-grained retrieval~\cite{wray2019jpose}. The second is MI-MM, an adaptation of MIL-NCE~\cite{miech2020milnce} with a multi-instance MaxMargin loss and S3D features, which serves as an explicit comparison point for the challenge. Both models operate with binary relevance objectives and do not exploit the soft-label matrices.

Scaling towards larger video foundation models has pushed the limits of multimodal understanding. Wang et al.~presented InternVideo2, which combines masked video pretraining, contrastive visual-textual alignment, and instruction-based fine-tuning, achieving state-of-the-art results on more than sixty evaluation benchmarks~\cite{wang2024internvideo2}. In the specific context of the EK-100 MIR challenge, Wang, Wang, and Chau proposed a symmetric multiple similarity loss that incorporates the soft-label relevance matrices directly into the cost computation, applied symmetrically over visual and textual representations~\cite{wang2024symmetricmultisimilaritylossepickitchens100}. This approach, built on LAVILA as the base encoder, represents one of the most explicit attempts to directly optimize the nDCG and mAP metrics of the competition.

In parallel, the visual retrieval community has developed loss functions that directly optimize ranking metrics. Engilberge et al.~proposed SoDeep, an auxiliary network that learns to approximate the sorting operation to make mAP and Recall computation differentiable, successfully applying it to cross-modal text-image retrieval~\cite{engilberge2019sodeep}. Brown et al.~introduced Smooth-AP, a sigmoidal approximation of Average Precision that enables its direct large-scale optimization in image retrieval~\cite{brown2020smoothap}. Despite these advances, this family of methods has not been systematically explored in the context of video-text retrieval with graded relevance, which represents a direct opportunity for the present work.

\subsection*{Identified gaps and project focus}

Despite the advances described, important limitations remain. Most video-text retrieval methods were designed for third-person video with binary relevance annotations, without considering the multi-instance structure of the egocentric scenario. Models specialized for first-person video, such as EgoVLP and LAVILA, employ loss functions that only partially leverage the graded relevance matrices: EgoNCE uses lexical overlap as a surrogate for true semantic similarity, while LAVILA applies standard contrastive learning without distinguishing degrees of relevance. Approaches that do incorporate soft labels, such as that of Wang et al.~\cite{wang2024symmetricmultisimilaritylossepickitchens100}, do so as a fine-tuning stage over representations pretrained with binary objectives. This project seeks to close this gap by developing a multimodal retrieval model that learns shared semantic representations for egocentric video segments and textual descriptions, explicitly integrating the soft-label relevance matrices from the training process itself, with the goal of directly maximizing the nDCG and mAP metrics of the EPIC-KITCHENS-100 MIR challenge.
